\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}

Mental health NLP models achieve high benchmark accuracy on depression and suicidal ideation tasks, yet growing evidence suggests they exploit \textit{shortcut cues}---spurious correlations between surface-level textual features and diagnostic labels---rather than clinically meaningful signal. We present a systematic three-stage audit of gender-correlated shortcut learning in frozen DistilRoBERTa-base representations, using a gender-balanced dataset of 32,200 Reddit posts (16,100 male, 16,100 female) with strong self-disclosure gender labels and binary mental health condition labels.

In \textbf{Stage~1}, we engineer 14 open-lexicon linguistic features and identify 7 whose mutual information with the label drops 24--40\% when conditioned on gender, establishing them as shortcut candidates. In \textbf{Stage~2}, we show that all 7 shortcuts are highly recoverable from frozen [CLS] embeddings (probing AUC 0.83--0.98), that gender itself is encoded at AUC = 0.959, and that removing shortcuts degrades a 14-feature classifier by 9.0\%. In \textbf{Stage~3}, causal mediation analysis confirms all 7 shortcuts are statistically significant mediators of the gender$\to$label association (combined $\sim$30\% of the indirect effect), yet INLP concept erasure reveals that shortcut information is encoded redundantly across many embedding dimensions and that erasing all 7 shortcuts changes label prediction AUC by only $-0.012$. Counterfactual prediction analysis shows female posts are 3$\times$ more likely to have their predictions flipped after shortcut erasure (20.7\% vs.\ 6.8\%), demonstrating gender-asymmetric shortcut reliance. Conditional independence tests confirm that gender carries predictive information beyond what these shortcuts explain, indicating that debiasing shortcuts alone is necessary but insufficient.

\section{Introduction}

Mental health disorders affect approximately one in eight people globally, yet access to timely diagnosis remains severely limited. This gap has motivated an explosion of NLP research aimed at automatic detection of depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation, and related conditions from social media text. Domain-adapted transformer models (MentalBERT, MentalRoBERTa; Ji et al., 2022) and prompted LLMs (GPT-4, Mental-LLM; Xu et al., 2024) routinely achieve F1 scores of 0.85--0.96 on benchmark Reddit datasets.

Yet this performance may be illusory. NLP classifiers are well-documented to exploit \textit{shortcuts}---features spuriously correlated with the target label in training data but not causally related to the clinical construct of interest (Wang \& Culotta, 2020; Clark et al., 2019). In mental health contexts, the stakes are high: a model that fires on hedging language or first-person pronoun density---both gender-correlated stylistic features---rather than on genuine psychopathological signal will fail to generalize and may encode discriminatory patterns.

Despite extensive documentation of shortcut learning in NLI, sentiment analysis, and toxicity detection, \textit{no systematic shortcut audit} has been conducted on mental health NLP representations. The evidence for shortcut reliance in this domain has been indirect: cross-platform generalization failure, MentalBERT's lexical dependency (Tang et al.), and performance gaps across demographic groups (Schuller et al., 2025). This paper addresses that gap directly.

\subsection{Contributions}

We present a three-stage empirical audit pipeline:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Shortcut Candidate Extraction} (Stage~1): We engineer 14 open-lexicon features and use mutual information decomposition conditioned on gender to identify 7 features where $>$24\% of the label-predictive MI is gender-mediated.

    \item \textbf{Shortcut Auditing} (Stage~2): We show via probing classifiers that all 7 shortcuts are recoverable from frozen DistilRoBERTa [CLS] embeddings (AUC 0.83--0.98), that gender is encoded at AUC = 0.959, and that SHAP attribution identifies first-person singular pronoun density as the dominant shortcut (41.7\% of total attribution mass).

    \item \textbf{Causal Disentanglement} (Stage~3): We apply causal mediation analysis, INLP concept erasure, counterfactual prediction analysis, and conditional independence testing. We find that shortcuts are significant mediators ($\sim$30\% of indirect effect) but that shortcut information is deeply redundant in the embedding space, that female predictions are 3$\times$ more sensitive to shortcut removal, and that gender carries predictive information beyond what shortcuts explain.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Mental Health NLP Models}

The field has progressed from lexical/feature-engineered approaches (LIWC-based logistic regression, SVM) to domain-adapted transformers. Ji et al.\ (2022) introduced MentalBERT and MentalRoBERTa, pretrained on 13.6 million Reddit mental health sentences. These models improve over general BERT on depression and suicidal ideation benchmarks but degrade substantially when domain-specific ``topic words'' are removed, suggesting heavy lexical reliance. General-purpose LLMs (GPT-4, FLAN-T5) have been evaluated on mental health tasks (Xu et al., 2024), approaching fine-tuned model performance while remaining opaque.

\subsection{Shortcut Learning in NLP}

Shortcut learning occurs when models exploit statistical regularities correlated with labels but not causally related to the task (Wang \& Culotta, 2020). In NLI, models exploit negation words and lexical overlap (Clark et al., 2019, 2020); sentiment classifiers learn that ``Spielberg'' predicts positive reviews; toxicity detectors associate identity group mentions with toxicity. Mechanisms include over-parameterization, dataset artifacts, and distributional shortcuts. The Less-Learn-Shortcut (LLS) framework (Huang et al., 2023) and Product-of-Experts approaches (Clark et al., 2020) represent mitigation strategies, but neither has been applied to mental health NLP.

\subsection{Gender Bias in NLP and Mental Health}

Gender bias is documented in word embeddings for depression prediction (arXiv:2212.07852), with different embeddings carrying different directional biases that propagate to downstream classification. Straw \& Callison-Burch (2020) documented performance biases across gender, race, and other demographics in mental health AI. Schuller et al.\ (2025) found that both age and gender shift classification outcomes in multilingual depression detection. Women express mental health differently from men---with more emotional disclosure, hedging, and relational language---and are over-represented in many mental health datasets, creating conditions for gender-mediated shortcut learning. However, the \textit{mechanism} by which gender-correlated features mediate model predictions has not been traced at the feature level.

\subsection{Probing, Attribution, and Causal Methods}

Probing classifiers (Belinkov, 2022) test whether specific properties are encoded in model representations. SHAP (Lundberg \& Lee, 2017) provides feature-level attribution. INLP (Ravfogel et al., 2020) removes concept information from embeddings via iterative nullspace projection. Causal mediation analysis (Imai et al., 2010) decomposes total effects into direct and indirect components. These tools have been applied individually across NLP but have not been combined into a systematic shortcut audit for mental health models.

\section{Methods}

Our audit pipeline consists of three stages, each building on the outputs of the previous stage.

\subsection{Stage 1: Shortcut Candidate Extraction}

\paragraph{Standalone predictive power.}
For each of the 14 features, we train a logistic regression classifier (5-fold stratified CV) to predict the binary mental health label using that feature alone, and record the AUC.

\paragraph{Mutual information decomposition.}
For each feature $f$, we compute:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item $I(f; Y)$: mutual information between feature $f$ and label $Y$
    \item $I(f; Y \mid G)$: conditional MI given gender $G$
    \item MI drop $= 1 - I(f; Y \mid G) / I(f; Y)$
\end{itemize}
A high MI drop indicates that a substantial fraction of the feature's label-predictive information is mediated by gender---marking it as a shortcut candidate. We flag features with MI drop $>$24\%.

\subsection{Stage 2: Shortcut Auditing}

\paragraph{[CLS] embedding extraction.}
We extract frozen [CLS] embeddings from DistilRoBERTa-base (no fine-tuning) for all 32,200 posts, yielding a $32{,}200 \times 768$ matrix.

\paragraph{Probing classifiers.}
For each shortcut feature, gender, and the binary label, we train logistic probing classifiers (5-fold stratified CV) on the [CLS] embeddings. High probing AUC indicates the property is encoded in the pre-trained representations.

\paragraph{SHAP attribution.}
We train a logistic regression model on the 7 shortcut features to predict the label and compute SHAP values for all 32,200 posts, identifying which shortcuts carry the most predictive weight.

\paragraph{Feature ablation.}
We measure the AUC of the full 14-feature model, then ablate each shortcut feature individually and all 7 simultaneously, recording AUC changes. We further stratify by gender to detect asymmetric reliance.

\subsection{Stage 3: Causal Disentanglement}

\paragraph{Structural causal model.}
We formalize the hypothesized causal graph:
\begin{verbatim}
    Gender (G) --> Shortcuts (S) --> Embedding (E) --> Prediction (Y-hat)
    Gender (G) --> Clinical Signal (C) --> Embedding (E) --> Prediction (Y-hat)
    Gender (G) ..............................-> Prediction (Y-hat)  [direct bias?]
\end{verbatim}

\paragraph{Causal mediation analysis.}
Following Imai et al.\ (2010), for each shortcut $M$ we estimate:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{ACME} (Average Causal Mediation Effect): the indirect effect $G \to M \to Y$
    \item \textbf{ADE} (Average Direct Effect): $G \to Y$ not through $M$
    \item \textbf{Proportion mediated}: ACME / (ACME + ADE)
\end{itemize}
with 1,000 bootstrap replications for 95\% confidence intervals.

\paragraph{INLP concept erasure.}
For each shortcut (binarized at the median), we apply Iterative Nullspace Projection (Ravfogel et al., 2020) with up to 15 iterations to remove the shortcut's linear direction from [CLS] embeddings. We then re-probe for (a) the erased concept, (b) gender, and (c) the label, measuring AUC changes. We also perform sequential erasure of all 7 shortcuts (removing 105 total directions) and direct gender erasure for comparison.

\paragraph{Counterfactual prediction analysis.}
We train a logistic label classifier on the original [CLS] embeddings, then apply it to each set of erased embeddings. Prediction flips (original $\neq$ counterfactual) are recorded and stratified by gender to measure asymmetric sensitivity.

\paragraph{Conditional independence tests.}
We test three d-separation hypotheses from the SCM via likelihood-ratio tests:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item $Y \perp G \mid S$ (7 shortcuts): Does gender add predictive power beyond shortcuts?
    \item $G \perp Y \mid S$: Does the label help predict gender beyond shortcuts?
    \item $Y \perp G \mid X_{14}$ (all 14 features): Does gender add power beyond all linguistic features?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Results}

\subsection{Stage 1: Shortcut Candidates}

Individual feature AUCs range from 0.50 (\texttt{question\_density}) to 0.64 (\texttt{fp\_singular}). The combined 14-feature logistic model achieves AUC = 0.709.

MI decomposition identifies 7 features with MI drop $>$24\% when conditioned on gender:

\texttt{fp\_singular} has both the highest MI drop (40.4\%) and the highest standalone AUC (0.640), making it the single most exploitable shortcut.

\paragraph{SHAP attribution.}
A logistic model trained on the 7 shortcut features achieves AUC = 0.693. SHAP analysis reveals \texttt{fp\_singular} accounts for 41.7\% of total $|\text{SHAP}|$ mass, followed by \texttt{negative\_emotion} (16.2\%) and \texttt{post\_length} (14.2\%).

\paragraph{Feature ablation.}
Removing all 7 shortcuts from the 14-feature model drops AUC from 0.709 to 0.645 ($-9.0$\%). Gender-stratified analysis reveals \texttt{fp\_singular} is female-biased (asymmetry = 0.010) and \texttt{post\_length} is male-biased (asymmetry = 0.005).

\subsection{Stage 3: Causal Analysis}

\paragraph{Mediation.}
The total gender $\to$ label effect is +0.259 on the probability scale (females are $\sim$26 percentage points more likely to receive a positive label). All 7 shortcuts are statistically significant mediators ($p < 0.05$ via bootstrap CI). \texttt{fp\_singular} alone mediates 13.5\% of this effect (ACME = +0.035, 95\% CI [+0.032, +0.038]). The 7 shortcuts combined account for approximately 30\% of the indirect path.

\paragraph{INLP concept erasure.}
INLP removes up to 15 linear directions per shortcut from the 768-dimensional [CLS] space. Key findings:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Per-shortcut erasure reduces concept probing AUC by 0.03--0.09 but \textit{never converges} (all hit the 15-iteration cap), indicating shortcut information is encoded redundantly across many dimensions.
    \item Gender probing AUC changes by $< -0.001$ after any individual shortcut erasure. Even after erasing all 7 shortcuts sequentially (105 directions), gender AUC drops only from 0.959 to 0.955 ($\Delta = -0.004$).
    \item Label probing AUC changes by $< -0.001$ per shortcut. After all 7 erasures: 0.798 $\to$ 0.787 ($\Delta = -0.012$).
    \item Direct gender erasure reduces gender AUC by $-0.080$ (0.959 $\to$ 0.879) and label AUC by only $-0.006$.
\end{itemize}

This reveals a critical asymmetry: shortcut information is a \textit{subset} of the much richer gender signal in [CLS], and the model's label predictions are robust to shortcut removal because gender information flows through many redundant pathways.

\paragraph{Counterfactual prediction flips.}
Applying the original label classifier to shortcut-erased embeddings produces gender-asymmetric prediction changes:

\begin{tabular}{lcccc}

Female predictions are approximately 3$\times$ more likely to flip than male predictions when shortcuts are removed. The combined shortcut erasure (13.8\% overall flip rate) exceeds direct gender erasure (10.0\%), indicating that the shortcut pathway carries \textit{more causal influence on predictions} than the gender signal alone.

\paragraph{Conditional independence.}
All three likelihood-ratio tests overwhelmingly reject independence ($p \approx 0$):

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item $Y \perp G \mid S_7$: \textbf{Rejected}. Adding gender to 7 shortcuts increases AUC by +0.041 (0.692 $\to$ 0.733).
    \item $G \perp Y \mid S_7$: \textbf{Rejected}. The label carries gender information beyond shortcuts.
    \item $Y \perp G \mid X_{14}$: \textbf{Rejected}. Gender adds +0.035 AUC even with all 14 features (0.709 $\to$ 0.744).
\end{enumerate}

This confirms that the 7 shortcuts (and even all 14 linguistic features) do \textit{not} fully mediate the gender--label association. Gender carries substantial predictive information through unmeasured channels.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{The Shortcut Landscape}

Our three-stage audit reveals a nuanced picture of shortcut learning in mental health NLP representations. The 7 MI-flagged shortcuts---particularly \texttt{fp\_singular} (first-person singular pronoun density)---are real and statistically significant mediators of the gender$\to$label association, collectively accounting for $\sim$30\% of the indirect effect. They are deeply encoded in pre-trained [CLS] representations (probing AUC 0.83--0.98), confirming that this information is available before any fine-tuning occurs.

However, the causal analysis reveals that these shortcuts are neither necessary nor sufficient for the model's gender-biased behavior:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Not necessary:} Erasing all 7 shortcuts (105 dimensions) reduces label AUC by only 0.012 and gender AUC by only 0.004. The model retains the ability to predict both.
    \item \textbf{Not sufficient:} Conditional independence tests show that gender carries additional predictive information beyond these shortcuts. The remaining $\sim$70\% of the indirect effect flows through unmeasured channels---likely vocabulary, topic, discourse structure, and other latent features correlated with gender.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Deep Redundancy of Gender Information}

The INLP analysis reveals a striking finding: shortcut information \textit{never converges} to unrecoverable levels within 15 iterations, and erasing 105 directions barely dents gender or label probing accuracy. This indicates that gender-correlated information is distributed across many more than 105 of the 768 embedding dimensions---a form of deep representational redundancy that makes targeted debiasing extremely challenging. Direct gender erasure is more effective ($\Delta_{\text{gender}} = -0.080$) but still leaves gender probing AUC at 0.879, well above chance.

\subsection{Gender-Asymmetric Shortcut Reliance}

The counterfactual analysis provides the most actionable finding: female predictions are approximately 3$\times$ more sensitive to shortcut removal than male predictions (20.7\% vs.\ 6.8\% flip rate for combined erasure). This asymmetry has direct fairness implications---it means that any classifier built on these representations will be disproportionately influenced by shortcut features for female-authored text, potentially leading to higher false-positive rates for women.

\subsection{Implications for Debiasing}

Our findings suggest that feature-level debiasing (removing specific linguistic shortcuts) is \textit{necessary but insufficient}. A comprehensive debiasing strategy must operate at the representation level---for example, adversarial training to prevent gender recovery from [CLS] embeddings, or counterfactual data augmentation that forces consistency across gender-swapped inputs. The conditional independence results further suggest that even perfect shortcut removal would leave substantial gender-mediated prediction differences, indicating that debiasing must also address the deeper distributional differences in how men and women write about mental health.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Single encoder:} We audit DistilRoBERTa-base only. Results may differ for MentalBERT, GPT-4, or other architectures.
    \item \textbf{Single dataset:} The Mindset corpus represents a specific Reddit population. Cross-dataset generalization remains untested.
    \item \textbf{Binary gender:} We use self-disclosed male/female labels. Non-binary and non-disclosing populations are not represented.
    \item \textbf{Frozen representations:} We audit pre-trained [CLS] without fine-tuning. Fine-tuning may amplify or attenuate shortcut reliance.
    \item \textbf{Linear probing:} We use logistic probes, which may underestimate nonlinear encoding of concepts.
    \item \textbf{Open lexicons:} Our 14 features are based on curated word lists rather than validated psycholinguistic instruments. Coverage and precision may differ from LIWC-22.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

We present the first systematic shortcut audit of transformer representations for mental health NLP, combining feature engineering, probing classifiers, SHAP attribution, causal mediation, INLP concept erasure, counterfactual analysis, and conditional independence testing into a unified three-stage pipeline.

Our key findings are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Seven gender-correlated linguistic features are identified as shortcut candidates via MI decomposition, with first-person singular pronoun density (\texttt{fp\_singular}) as the most exploitable (40.4\% MI drop, 13.5\% of gender$\to$label mediation).

    \item All shortcuts are deeply encoded in frozen DistilRoBERTa [CLS] representations (probing AUC 0.83--0.98), confirming that shortcut information is pre-baked, not fine-tuning-induced.

    \item Shortcut information is encoded with deep redundancy across embedding dimensions, making targeted erasure ineffective: removing 105 directions changes label AUC by only $-0.012$.

    \item Female predictions are 3$\times$ more sensitive to shortcut removal than male predictions, demonstrating gender-asymmetric shortcut reliance with direct fairness implications.

    \item Gender carries predictive information beyond what shortcuts explain, indicating that debiasing shortcuts alone is necessary but insufficient.
\end{enumerate}

These findings establish a concrete empirical foundation for representation-level debiasing interventions (adversarial training, counterfactual augmentation) in mental health NLP, which we plan to address in subsequent work.
