\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}

Mental health NLP classifiers routinely achieve high benchmark accuracy, yet growing evidence suggests they exploit \textit{shortcut cues}---surface-level textual features that are spuriously correlated with diagnostic labels via demographic confounds---rather than clinically meaningful signal. We ask: do transformer-based models learn to rely on gender-correlated writing-style features as predictive shortcuts, and if so, through what causal mechanism does this reliance produce gender-asymmetric predictions?

We present a five-stage audit pipeline applied to frozen DistilRoBERTa-base representations over a gender-balanced dataset of 32,200 Reddit posts (16,100 male, 16,100 female). In \textbf{Stage~1}, we engineer 18 open-lexicon linguistic features---14 candidate features plus 4 theoretically motivated controls---and identify 6 whose mutual information with the label drops 28--48\% when conditioned on gender, establishing them as shortcut candidates. In \textbf{Stage~2}, we show that all 6 shortcuts are highly recoverable from frozen [CLS] embeddings (probing AUC 0.83--0.998), that gender is encoded at AUC~=~0.959, and that \texttt{fp\_singular} (first-person singular pronouns) accounts for 51.3\% of SHAP attribution mass. In \textbf{Stage~3}, causal mediation analysis confirms that shortcuts are statistically significant mediators of the gender$\to$label association, while INLP concept erasure reveals deep redundancy in how shortcut information is distributed across embedding dimensions. Counterfactual prediction analysis shows gender-asymmetric sensitivity to shortcut removal, and conditional independence tests confirm that gender carries predictive information beyond what shortcuts explain. These findings establish that debiasing individual shortcuts is necessary but insufficient, and motivate representation-level interventions addressed in Stages~4--5.

\section{Introduction}

Mental health disorders affect approximately one in eight people globally, yet access to timely diagnosis remains severely limited. This gap has motivated an explosion of NLP research aimed at automatic detection of depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation, and related conditions from social media text. Domain-adapted transformer models (MentalBERT, MentalRoBERTa; Ji et al., 2022) and prompted LLMs (GPT-4, Mental-LLM; Xu et al., 2024) routinely achieve F1 scores of 0.85--0.96 on benchmark Reddit datasets.

Yet this performance may be illusory. Shortcut learning---the exploitation of surface-level features spuriously correlated with labels but not causally related to the target construct---is well-documented in NLI, sentiment analysis, and toxicity detection (Geirhos et al., 2020; McCoy et al., 2019; Du et al., 2023). In mental health contexts, the stakes are uniquely high: a model that fires on first-person pronoun density or hedging language---both gender-correlated stylistic features---rather than on genuine psychopathological signal will fail to generalize across demographics and may produce systematically biased clinical decisions.

Recent work has begun to address dataset-level bias in medical AI. G-AUDIT (Drenkow et al., 2025) provides a modality-agnostic framework for flagging attributes with high utility and detectability as shortcut risks. However, such dataset-level audits identify \textit{that} a shortcut risk exists without tracing \textit{how} a trained model exploits it or \textit{whom} it harms. In the mental health NLP domain specifically, evidence for shortcut reliance remains indirect: cross-platform generalization failure, MentalBERT's lexical dependency (Tang et al.), and performance gaps across demographic groups (Schuller et al., 2025; Harrigian et al., 2023).

This paper provides a mechanistic account of how gender bias enters mental health predictions through writing-style shortcuts.

\subsection{Scope and Objective}

\textbf{Scope.} We audit frozen (non-fine-tuned) DistilRoBERTa-base representations on a single gender-balanced mental health dataset (Mindset corpus, 32,200 Reddit posts). We focus on \textit{gender} as the protected attribute and on \textit{writing-style features} (pronoun usage, hedging, emotional language, post length) as the candidate shortcut mechanism. We do not claim to cover all sources of bias, all models, or all datasets.

\textbf{Objective.} We extend dataset-level shortcut auditing (Drenkow et al., 2025) in three directions: (1)~we move from dataset-level to \textit{representation-level} auditing, asking not whether shortcuts \textit{could} be exploited but whether a trained model \textit{has} exploited them; (2)~we focus on settings where the protected attribute influences predictions not through explicit metadata but through \textit{correlated writing-style proxies} that are entangled with legitimate clinical signal; and (3)~we provide \textit{causal mechanism analysis}---per-feature mediation decomposition, concept erasure, and gender-stratified counterfactual interventions---that traces exactly how bias flows from demographic attribute to clinical prediction.

\subsection{Research Questions}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em, labelindent=0em]
    \item[RQ1] Do mental health NLP classifiers learn gender-correlated writing-style shortcuts, and if so, do these shortcuts \textit{causally mediate} the relationship between gender and clinical predictions?
    \item[RQ1a] Which linguistic features exhibit significant mutual information with both gender and clinical labels---functioning as ``dual-use'' shortcuts?
    \item[RQ1b] To what extent are these shortcuts encoded in transformer representations, and how much do they contribute to classification decisions?
    \item[RQ1c] What fraction of the total gender$\to$prediction effect is \textit{causally transmitted} through each shortcut feature?
    \item[RQ1d] Does removing shortcut information from embeddings reduce gender bias, and does this reduction affect male and female subgroups asymmetrically?
\end{description}

\subsection{Hypotheses}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em, labelindent=0em]
    \item[H1 (Shortcut Encoding)] Gender-correlated linguistic features (e.g., first-person singular pronouns, hedging, emotional language) are deeply encoded in frozen transformer [CLS] embeddings and disproportionately drive clinical predictions.
    \item[H2 (Causal Mediation)] A significant fraction of the gender$\to$prediction association is \textit{indirectly mediated} through writing-style shortcuts rather than through legitimate clinical signal---i.e., ACME $\neq 0$ for multiple shortcut features.
    \item[H3 (Asymmetric Impact)] The reliance on shortcuts harms gender subgroups unequally: removing shortcut information changes predictions more for one gender than the other.
    \item[H4 (Incomplete Mediation)] Shortcuts do not \textit{fully} mediate the gender$\to$prediction path---gender retains residual predictive power even after conditioning on all shortcut features, implying additional unmeasured confounds.
\end{description}

\subsection{Contributions}

We present a five-stage empirical audit pipeline (Stages~1--3 reported here; Stages~4--5 are ongoing):

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Shortcut Candidate Extraction} (Stage~1): We engineer 18 open-lexicon features---14 candidates plus 4 theoretically motivated controls---and use mutual information decomposition conditioned on gender to identify 6 features where $>$28\% of the label-predictive MI is gender-mediated.

    \item \textbf{Shortcut Auditing} (Stage~2): We show via probing classifiers that all 6 shortcuts are recoverable from frozen DistilRoBERTa [CLS] embeddings (AUC 0.83--0.998), that gender is encoded at AUC~=~0.959, and that SHAP attribution identifies first-person singular pronoun density as the dominant shortcut (51.3\% of total attribution mass).

    \item \textbf{Causal Disentanglement} (Stage~3): We apply causal mediation analysis, INLP concept erasure, counterfactual prediction analysis, and conditional independence testing. We find that shortcuts are significant mediators, that shortcut information is deeply redundant in the embedding space, that female predictions are more sensitive to shortcut removal, and that gender carries predictive information beyond what shortcuts explain.

    \item \textbf{Debiasing Interventions} (Stage~4, ongoing): Adversarial gender removal and INLP-debiased embedding classifiers.

    \item \textbf{Evaluation} (Stage~5, ongoing): Pre/post-debiasing comparison of fairness metrics, clinical accuracy, and generalization.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Shortcut Learning in NLP}

Shortcut learning occurs when models exploit statistical regularities correlated with labels but not causally related to the task (Geirhos et al., 2020). The phenomenon is pervasive: NLI models exploit negation words and lexical overlap (McCoy et al., 2019; Clark et al., 2019), sentiment classifiers learn author identity as proxy for polarity, and toxicity detectors associate identity group mentions with toxicity. Du et al.\ (2023) provide a comprehensive survey of shortcut learning in LLMs across NLU tasks. Mitigation strategies include Product-of-Experts ensembles (Clark et al., 2020), the Less-Learn-Shortcut framework (Huang et al., 2023), and counterfactual data augmentation, but none have been applied to mental health NLP.

\subsection{Dataset Bias Auditing in Medical AI}

G-AUDIT (Drenkow et al., 2025) represents the closest methodological precedent to our work. It proposes a modality-agnostic dataset auditing framework using mutual information to quantify attribute \textit{utility} ($\text{MI}(A; Y)$) and \textit{detectability} ($\text{MI}(A; \hat{A})$), flagging attributes with high values on both axes as shortcut risks. Applied across skin lesion images, EHR text, and ICU tabular data, G-AUDIT successfully identifies subtle biases overlooked by qualitative methods. Our work extends this paradigm in three directions: (1)~we move from dataset-level to \textit{representation-level} auditing, asking not whether shortcuts \textit{could} be exploited but whether a trained encoder \textit{has} encoded them; (2)~we focus on settings where the protected attribute (gender) influences predictions through correlated writing-style proxies entangled with clinical signal, rather than through retrievable metadata; and (3)~we provide causal mechanism analysis---per-feature mediation decomposition, concept erasure, and gender-stratified counterfactual interventions---that traces exactly how bias flows from demographic attribute to clinical prediction. Where G-AUDIT is the metal detector, our pipeline is the archaeological excavation.

\subsection{Mental Health NLP Models}

The field has progressed from lexical/feature-engineered approaches (LIWC-based logistic regression, SVM) to domain-adapted transformers. Ji et al.\ (2022) introduced MentalBERT and MentalRoBERTa, pretrained on 13.6 million Reddit mental health sentences. These models improve over general BERT on depression and suicidal ideation benchmarks but degrade substantially when domain-specific ``topic words'' are removed, suggesting heavy lexical reliance. General-purpose LLMs (GPT-4, FLAN-T5) have been evaluated on mental health tasks (Xu et al., 2024), approaching fine-tuned model performance while remaining opaque.

\subsection{Gender, Language, and Mental Health}

Sociolinguistic research has long documented gender differences in language use. Women use more first-person singular pronouns, emotional disclosure, hedging, and relational language (Newman et al., 2008; Schwartz et al., 2013). Critically, many of these same features are also associated with depression and anxiety (Rude et al., 2004), creating a confound: features that appear clinically informative may in fact be capturing gender-linked writing style. Straw \& Callison-Burch (2020) documented performance biases across gender and race in mental health AI. Harrigian et al.\ (2023) showed that mental health classifiers exploit demographic proxies, measuring performance drops when confounds are controlled. Schuller et al.\ (2025) found that both age and gender shift classification outcomes in multilingual depression detection. However, the \textit{mechanism} by which gender-correlated features mediate model predictions has not been traced at the feature level.

\subsection{Probing, Attribution, and Causal Methods}

Probing classifiers (Belinkov, 2022) test whether specific properties are encoded in model representations. SHAP (Lundberg \& Lee, 2017) provides feature-level attribution. INLP (Ravfogel et al., 2020) removes concept information from embeddings via iterative nullspace projection; LEACE (Belrose et al., 2023) provides a closed-form alternative. Causal mediation analysis (Imai et al., 2010) decomposes total effects into direct and indirect components. Vig et al.\ (2020) applied mediation analysis to attention heads in GPT-2. Elazar \& Goldberg (2018) showed adversarial demographic removal is unreliable, motivating multi-method triangulation. These tools have been applied individually across NLP but have not been combined into a systematic shortcut audit for mental health representations.

\section{Methods}

Our audit pipeline consists of three stages, each building on the outputs of the previous stage.

\subsection{Stage 1: Shortcut Candidate Extraction}

\paragraph{Standalone predictive power.}
For each of the 18 features, we train a logistic regression classifier (5-fold stratified CV) to predict the binary mental health label using that feature alone, and record the AUC.

\paragraph{Mutual information decomposition.}
For each feature $f$, we compute:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item $I(f; Y)$: mutual information between feature $f$ and label $Y$
    \item $I(f; Y \mid G)$: conditional MI given gender $G$
    \item MI drop $= 1 - I(f; Y \mid G) / I(f; Y)$
\end{itemize}
A high MI drop indicates that a substantial fraction of the feature's label-predictive information is mediated by gender---marking it as a shortcut candidate. We flag features with MI drop $>$24\%.

\paragraph{Control feature validation.}
The 4 control features serve as a methodological check. If the MI decomposition is sound, controls should exhibit \textit{low} MI drop ($<$10\%), confirming that the procedure flags gender-confounded features rather than all predictive features. Partial validation (some controls passing, others failing) itself provides insight into the depth of gender confounding in the feature space.

\subsection{Stage 2: Shortcut Auditing}

\paragraph{[CLS] embedding extraction.}
We extract frozen [CLS] embeddings from DistilRoBERTa-base (no fine-tuning) for all 32,200 posts, yielding a $32{,}200 \times 768$ matrix.

\paragraph{Probing classifiers.}
For each shortcut feature, gender, and the binary label, we train logistic probing classifiers (5-fold stratified CV) on the [CLS] embeddings. High probing AUC indicates the property is encoded in the pre-trained representations.

\paragraph{SHAP attribution.}
We train a logistic regression model on the 6 shortcut features to predict the label and compute SHAP values for all 32,200 posts, identifying which shortcuts carry the most predictive weight.

\paragraph{Feature ablation.}
We measure the AUC of the full 18-feature model, then ablate each shortcut feature individually and all 6 simultaneously, recording AUC changes. We further stratify by gender to detect asymmetric reliance.

\subsection{Stage 3: Causal Disentanglement}

\paragraph{Structural causal model.}
We formalize the hypothesized causal graph:
\begin{verbatim}
    Gender (G) --> Shortcuts (S) --> Embedding (E) --> Prediction (Y-hat)
    Gender (G) --> Clinical Signal (C) --> Embedding (E) --> Prediction (Y-hat)
    Gender (G) ..............................-> Prediction (Y-hat)  [direct bias?]
\end{verbatim}

\paragraph{Causal mediation analysis.}
Following Imai et al.\ (2010), for each shortcut $M$ we estimate:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{ACME} (Average Causal Mediation Effect): the indirect effect $G \to M \to Y$
    \item \textbf{ADE} (Average Direct Effect): $G \to Y$ not through $M$
    \item \textbf{Proportion mediated}: ACME / (ACME + ADE)
\end{itemize}
with 1,000 bootstrap replications for 95\% confidence intervals.

\paragraph{INLP concept erasure.}
For each shortcut (binarized at the median), we apply Iterative Nullspace Projection (Ravfogel et al., 2020) with up to 15 iterations to remove the shortcut's linear direction from [CLS] embeddings. We then re-probe for (a) the erased concept, (b) gender, and (c) the label, measuring AUC changes. We also perform sequential erasure of all 6 shortcuts and direct gender erasure for comparison.

\paragraph{Counterfactual prediction analysis.}
We train a logistic label classifier on the original [CLS] embeddings, then apply it to each set of erased embeddings. Prediction flips (original $\neq$ counterfactual) are recorded and stratified by gender to measure asymmetric sensitivity.

\paragraph{Conditional independence tests.}
We test three d-separation hypotheses from the SCM via likelihood-ratio tests:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item $Y \perp G \mid S$ (6 shortcuts): Does gender add predictive power beyond shortcuts?
    \item $G \perp Y \mid S$: Does the label help predict gender beyond shortcuts?
    \item $Y \perp G \mid X_{18}$ (all 18 features): Does gender add power beyond all linguistic features?
\end{enumerate}

However, \texttt{past\_tense} (40.0\%) and \texttt{absolutist} (32.7\%) are unexpectedly flagged, revealing that gender confounding in mental health text is deeper than anticipated---even features not typically considered gendered carry substantial gender-mediated predictive information. These unexpected control failures are themselves informative (see Discussion).

\paragraph{SHAP attribution.}
A logistic model trained on the 6 shortcut features achieves AUC = 0.680. SHAP analysis reveals \texttt{fp\_singular} accounts for 51.3\% of total $|\text{SHAP}|$ mass, followed by \texttt{emotional\_feeling} (17.1\%) and \texttt{post\_length} (15.0\%). \paragraph{Feature ablation.}
Removing all 6 shortcuts from the 18-feature model drops AUC from 0.7129 to 0.6839 ($\Delta = -0.0291$, $-4.1$\%). Gender-stratified analysis reveals \texttt{fp\_singular} is female-biased (asymmetry = +0.0068, higher reliance for female posts) and \texttt{post\_length} is male-biased (asymmetry = +0.0042).

\paragraph{INLP concept erasure.}
INLP removes up to 15 linear directions per shortcut from the 768-dimensional [CLS] space. Key findings:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Per-shortcut erasure reduces concept probing AUC by 0.03--0.09 but \textit{never converges} (all hit the 15-iteration cap), indicating shortcut information is encoded redundantly across many dimensions.
    \item Gender probing AUC changes by $< -0.001$ after any individual shortcut erasure. Even after erasing all 6 shortcuts sequentially ($\leq$90 directions), gender AUC drops only from 0.959 to $\sim$0.955 ($\Delta \approx -0.004$).
    \item Label probing AUC changes by $< -0.001$ per shortcut. After all 6 erasures: 0.798 $\to$ $\sim$0.787 ($\Delta \approx -0.012$).
    \item Direct gender erasure reduces gender AUC by $-0.080$ (0.959 $\to$ 0.879) and label AUC by only $-0.006$.
\end{itemize}

This reveals a critical asymmetry: shortcut information is a \textit{subset} of the much richer gender signal in [CLS], and the model's label predictions are robust to shortcut removal because gender information flows through many redundant pathways.

\end{table}

Female predictions are approximately 3$\times$ more likely to flip than male predictions when shortcuts are removed. The combined shortcut erasure ($\sim$13\% overall flip rate) exceeds direct gender erasure (10.0\%), indicating that the shortcut pathway carries \textit{more causal influence on predictions} than the gender signal alone.

\paragraph{Conditional independence.}
All three likelihood-ratio tests overwhelmingly reject independence ($p \approx 0$):

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item $Y \perp G \mid S_6$: \textbf{Rejected}. Adding gender to 6 shortcuts increases AUC by $\sim$+0.04 (0.680 $\to$ $\sim$0.72).
    \item $G \perp Y \mid S_6$: \textbf{Rejected}. The label carries gender information beyond shortcuts.
    \item $Y \perp G \mid X_{18}$: \textbf{Rejected}. Gender adds $\sim$+0.03 AUC even with all 18 features (0.713 $\to$ $\sim$0.74).
\end{enumerate}

This confirms that the 6 shortcuts (and even all 18 linguistic features) do \textit{not} fully mediate the gender--label association. Gender carries substantial predictive information through unmeasured channels.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{The Shortcut Landscape}

Our three-stage audit reveals a nuanced picture of shortcut learning in mental health NLP representations. The 6 MI-flagged shortcuts---particularly \texttt{fp\_singular} (first-person singular pronoun density, 51.3\% of SHAP mass)---are real and statistically significant mediators of the gender$\to$label association, collectively accounting for $\sim$25--30\% of the indirect effect. They are deeply encoded in pre-trained [CLS] representations (probing AUC 0.83--0.998), confirming that this information is available \textit{before any fine-tuning occurs}.

However, the causal analysis reveals that these shortcuts are neither necessary nor sufficient for the model's gender-biased behavior:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Not necessary:} Erasing all 6 shortcuts ($\leq$90 dimensions) reduces label AUC by only $\sim$0.012 and gender AUC by only $\sim$0.004. The model retains the ability to predict both.
    \item \textbf{Not sufficient:} Conditional independence tests show that gender carries additional predictive information beyond these shortcuts. The remaining $\sim$70\% of the indirect effect flows through unmeasured channels---likely vocabulary, topic, discourse structure, and other latent features correlated with gender.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Control Features Reveal Pervasive Gender Confounding}

The 4 control features provide a methodological validation and an unexpected finding. \texttt{negation} (0.6\% MI drop) and \texttt{cognitive\_processing} (7.9\%) behave as expected: they predict mental health labels without gender confounding, confirming that the MI decomposition distinguishes shortcut from genuine features. However, \texttt{past\_tense} (40.0\%) and \texttt{absolutist} (32.7\%) are unexpectedly flagged. Both features are documented correlates of depression (Al-Mosaiwi \& Johnstone, 2018; Tausczik \& Pennebaker, 2010) but were chosen as controls because they were not \textit{a priori} expected to be gendered.

The failure of these controls suggests that gender confounding in mental health text is even deeper than anticipated. Features that seem clinically grounded may still carry gender-mediated signal because \textit{men and women describe distress using systematically different linguistic strategies}. This depth of confounding strengthens the case for representation-level interventions rather than feature-level fixes.

\subsection{Deep Redundancy of Gender Information}

The INLP analysis reveals a striking finding: shortcut information \textit{never converges} to unrecoverable levels within 15 iterations, and erasing up to 90 directions barely dents gender or label probing accuracy. This indicates that gender-correlated information is distributed across many more than 90 of the 768 embedding dimensions---a form of deep representational redundancy that makes targeted debiasing extremely challenging. Direct gender erasure is more effective ($\Delta_{\text{gender}} = -0.080$) but still leaves gender probing AUC at 0.879, well above chance.

\subsection{Gender-Asymmetric Shortcut Reliance}

The counterfactual analysis provides the most actionable finding: female predictions are approximately 3$\times$ more sensitive to shortcut removal than male predictions ($\sim$20\% vs.\ $\sim$6\% flip rate for combined erasure). This asymmetry has direct fairness implications---it means that any classifier built on these representations will be disproportionately influenced by shortcut features for female-authored text, potentially leading to higher false-positive rates for women. The SHAP gender-stratified analysis corroborates this: \texttt{fp\_singular} shows asymmetry of +0.0068 (higher reliance for female posts).

\subsection{Comparison to Dataset-Level Auditing}

Our representation-level approach complements dataset-level auditing frameworks such as G-AUDIT (Drenkow et al., 2025). Where G-AUDIT evaluates model performance across predefined demographic slices to detect disparities, our pipeline traces the \textit{mechanism} by which a specific protected attribute (gender) enters predictions---from surface-level writing patterns through embedding representations to downstream predictions. The two approaches answer different questions: G-AUDIT asks ``Does performance differ across groups?'' while our pipeline asks ``\textit{Why} does it differ, and through which representational pathways?''

\subsection{Implications for Debiasing}

Our findings suggest that feature-level debiasing (removing specific linguistic shortcuts) is \textit{necessary but insufficient}. Removing all 6 shortcuts reduces the 18-feature model AUC by only 4.1\%, and the embedding-level erasure of these same shortcuts barely affects probing accuracy. A comprehensive debiasing strategy must operate at the representation level---for example, adversarial training to prevent gender recovery from [CLS] embeddings, or counterfactual data augmentation that forces consistency across gender-swapped inputs. The conditional independence results further suggest that even perfect shortcut removal would leave substantial gender-mediated prediction differences.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Single encoder:} We audit DistilRoBERTa-base only. Results may differ for MentalBERT, GPT-4, or other architectures.
    \item \textbf{Single dataset:} The Mindset corpus represents a specific Reddit population. Cross-dataset generalization remains untested.
    \item \textbf{Binary gender:} We use self-disclosed male/female labels. Non-binary and non-disclosing populations are not represented.
    \item \textbf{Frozen representations:} We audit pre-trained [CLS] without fine-tuning. Fine-tuning may amplify or attenuate shortcut reliance.
    \item \textbf{Linear probing:} We use logistic probes, which may underestimate nonlinear encoding of concepts.
    \item \textbf{Open lexicons:} Our 18 features are based on curated word lists rather than validated psycholinguistic instruments. Coverage and precision may differ from LIWC-22.
    \item \textbf{Stage 3 pending:} Mediation, INLP, and counterfactual results reported here are preliminary; re-execution with the updated 6-shortcut set is in progress.
\end{itemize}

% ============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}

We present the first systematic shortcut audit of transformer representations for mental health NLP, combining feature engineering (18 open-lexicon features with 4 controls), MI-based shortcut detection, probing classifiers, SHAP attribution, causal mediation, INLP concept erasure, counterfactual analysis, and conditional independence testing into a unified five-stage pipeline.

Our key findings are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Six gender-correlated linguistic features are identified as shortcut candidates via MI decomposition, with first-person singular pronoun density (\texttt{fp\_singular}) as the most exploitable (37.5\% MI drop, 51.3\% of SHAP attribution mass, $\sim$13.5\% of gender$\to$label mediation).

    \item All shortcuts are deeply encoded in frozen DistilRoBERTa [CLS] representations (probing AUC 0.83--0.998), confirming that shortcut information is pre-baked, not fine-tuning-induced.

    \item Two of four control features unexpectedly exhibit high MI drop (\texttt{past\_tense} 40.0\%, \texttt{absolutist} 32.7\%), revealing that gender confounding in mental health language is deeper than surface-level writing style.

    \item Shortcut information is encoded with deep redundancy across embedding dimensions, making targeted erasure ineffective: removing $\leq$90 directions changes label AUC by only $\sim$$-0.012$.

    \item Female predictions are 3$\times$ more sensitive to shortcut removal than male predictions, demonstrating gender-asymmetric shortcut reliance with direct fairness implications.

    \item Gender carries predictive information beyond what shortcuts (or all 18 features) explain, indicating that feature-level debiasing alone is necessary but insufficient.
\end{enumerate}

Stages 4 (debiasing interventions: adversarial training, counterfactual augmentation) and 5 (comprehensive fairness evaluation) are currently in progress. These findings establish a concrete empirical foundation for representation-level debiasing in mental health NLP.
