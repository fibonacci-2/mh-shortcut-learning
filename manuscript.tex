\title{How Gender Bias Enters Clinical Predictions:\\Tracing Shortcut Pathways in Mental Health NLP}

\begin{abstract}
Mental health NLP classifiers may exploit \textit{shortcut features}---surface-level cues spuriously correlated with diagnostic labels via demographic confounds---rather than clinically meaningful signal.
We present a three-block audit of frozen DistilRoBERTa representations on a gender-balanced Reddit corpus (32,200 posts; 18 open-lexicon features).
\textbf{Identify:} mutual information decomposition flags 6 of 14 candidate features whose label-predictive power drops 28--48\% when conditioned on gender; 2 of 4 control features are unexpectedly flagged, revealing pervasive confounding.
\textbf{Locate:} layer-wise probing shows all shortcuts are encoded by the first transformer layer (AUC $\geq 0.86$), tracking the gender curve more closely than the label curve.
\textbf{Quantify:} SHAP analysis attributes 51.3\% of predictive mass to first-person singular pronouns; causal mediation confirms shortcuts significantly mediate the gender$\to$label association ($\sim$13.5\% via \texttt{fp\_singular} alone); gender-stratified ablation shows female predictions are $3\times$ more sensitive to shortcut removal.
These findings establish that pre-trained representations encode gender-mediated shortcuts before any fine-tuning, with asymmetric fairness consequences.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Mental health disorders affect approximately one in eight people globally, yet access to timely diagnosis remains limited.
NLP models for automatic detection of depression, anxiety, and suicidal ideation from social media text \citep{coppersmith,cohan} routinely achieve high benchmark accuracy, with domain-adapted transformers such as MentalBERT \citep{ji2022mentalbert} and prompted LLMs \citep{xu2024mentalllm} reaching F1 scores of 0.85--0.96 on Reddit datasets.

Yet this performance may be illusory.
Shortcut learning---the exploitation of surface-level features spuriously correlated with labels---is well-documented in NLI, sentiment analysis, and toxicity detection \citep{geirhos2020shortcut,mccoy2019right,du2023shortcut}.
In mental health contexts, the risk is acute: sociolinguistic research documents that women use more first-person pronouns, hedging, and emotional disclosure \citep{newman2008gender,schwartz2013personality}---features that also correlate with depression \citep{rude2004language}---creating a gender$\to$writing-style$\to$label confound.
A model that fires on pronoun density rather than psychopathological signal will produce systematically biased clinical decisions.

Dataset-level auditing frameworks such as G-AUDIT \citep{drenkow2025gaudit} can flag attributes with high utility and detectability as shortcut risks, but they identify \textit{that} a risk exists without tracing \textit{how} representations encode it or \textit{whom} it harms.
We provide this mechanistic account.

\paragraph{Research questions.}
\begin{itemize}
\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
\item[\textbf{RQ1}] Which writing-style features function as gender-mediated shortcuts for mental health classification?
\item[\textbf{RQ2}] Are these shortcuts encoded in pre-trained transformer representations, and at what depth?
\item[\textbf{RQ3}] How much of the gender$\to$prediction pathway flows through identifiable shortcuts?
\item[\textbf{RQ4}] Is the model's reliance on shortcuts gender-asymmetric?
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We present a three-block audit---\textsc{Identify}, \textsc{Locate}, \textsc{Quantify}---applied to frozen DistilRoBERTa-base on a gender-balanced mental health dataset:
(1)~an MI-based shortcut detection method with built-in control validation that identifies 6 gender-confounded features and reveals deeper-than-expected confounding;
(2)~layer-wise probing showing shortcuts are encoded from the first transformer layer, tracking gender more than the label;
(3)~SHAP and causal mediation evidence that \texttt{fp\_singular} alone carries 51.3\% of shortcut attribution and mediates $\sim$13.5\% of the gender$\to$label effect; and
(4)~gender-stratified ablation demonstrating $3\times$ asymmetric sensitivity for female-authored posts.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Shortcut learning in NLP.}
Shortcut learning occurs when models exploit statistical regularities correlated with labels but not causally related to the task \citep{geirhos2020shortcut}.
NLI models exploit lexical overlap \citep{mccoy2019right}, sentiment classifiers learn author identity, and toxicity detectors fire on identity-group mentions.
\citet{du2023shortcut} survey shortcut learning in LLMs across NLU tasks.
Mitigation strategies include Product-of-Experts ensembles \citep{clark2020poe} and counterfactual data augmentation, but none have been applied to mental health NLP.

\paragraph{Dataset-level bias auditing.}
G-AUDIT \citep{drenkow2025gaudit} proposes a modality-agnostic framework using MI to flag attributes with high utility and detectability as shortcut risks.
Our work extends this paradigm from dataset-level to \textit{representation-level} auditing: we trace not just whether shortcuts exist but how models encode them across layers, which carry the most causal weight, and whom they harm.

\paragraph{Gender, language, and mental health NLP.}
Women use more first-person singular pronouns, emotional disclosure, and hedging \citep{newman2008gender,schwartz2013personality}---features also correlated with depression \citep{rude2004language}---creating a confound.
MentalBERT \citep{ji2022mentalbert} degrades substantially when topic words are removed, suggesting lexical reliance.
\citet{harrigian2023characterization} showed mental health classifiers exploit demographic proxies; \citet{schuller2025bias} found gender shifts classification outcomes in multilingual settings.
Yet the \textit{mechanism} by which gender-correlated features mediate predictions has not been traced at the feature level.

\paragraph{Probing, attribution, and causal methods.}
Probing classifiers \citep{belinkov2022probing} test whether properties are linearly encoded in representations.
SHAP \citep{lundberg2017unified} provides feature-level attribution.
Causal mediation analysis \citep{imai2010general} decomposes effects into direct and indirect components; \citet{vig2020mediation} applied it to GPT-2 attention heads.
These tools have been applied individually but not combined into a systematic shortcut audit for mental health representations.

\paragraph{Dataset.}
We use a curated subset of the Mindset corpus \citep{koloski2024mindset} (Reddit mental health and control subreddits), with binary gender labels from high-precision regex matching on explicit self-disclosure (e.g., ``I'm a 24F'').

\paragraph{Features.}
We engineer 18 open-lexicon linguistic features---14 candidates plus 4 \textit{controls}---replacing proprietary LIWC-22 categories.
Each feature is a normalized token-count density or a log-transformed count (full inventory in Table~\ref{tab:features}, Appendix~\ref{sec:features}).
Controls (\texttt{absolutist}, \texttt{past\_tense}, \texttt{negation}, \texttt{cognitive\_processing}) were chosen from psycholinguistic literature as clinically relevant but not \textit{a priori} gendered, providing a built-in validation of the MI decomposition.

\section{Methods}

Our audit has three blocks---\textsc{Identify}, \textsc{Locate}, \textsc{Quantify}---each answering a specific research question.

\subsection{\textsc{Identify}: Shortcut Detection (RQ1)}

\paragraph{Standalone predictive power.}
For each of the 18 features we train a logistic regression classifier (5-fold stratified CV) to predict the binary label using that feature alone, recording the AUC.

\paragraph{Mutual information decomposition.}
For each feature $f$ we compute $I(f; Y)$, the conditional $I(f; Y \mid G)$, and the \textit{MI drop} $= 1 - I(f; Y \mid G)/I(f; Y)$.
A high MI drop indicates that a substantial share of the feature's label information is gender-mediated.
We flag features with MI drop $>$24\%.

\paragraph{Control validation.}
The 4 controls serve as a sanity check: if the decomposition is sound, controls should show low MI drop ($<$10\%), confirming that the procedure flags gender-confounded features rather than all predictive features.

\subsection{\textsc{Locate}: Representation Probing (RQ2)}

\paragraph{[CLS] extraction.}
We extract frozen [CLS] embeddings from DistilRoBERTa-base (no fine-tuning) for all 32,200 posts, yielding a $32{,}200 \times 768$ matrix.

\paragraph{Final-layer probing.}
For each shortcut, gender, and the binary label, we train logistic probing classifiers (5-fold stratified CV) on the final-layer [CLS] embeddings.

\paragraph{Layer-wise probing.}
To trace \textit{when} shortcuts are encoded, we extract [CLS] from all 7 extraction points (static embedding layer + 6 transformer layers) and repeat the probing protocol at each depth.
We compare shortcut, gender, and label curves to test whether shortcuts track the gender or the label encoding trajectory.

\subsection{\textsc{Quantify}: Attribution \& Mediation (RQ3--4)}

\paragraph{SHAP attribution.}
We train a logistic model on the 6 shortcut features and compute SHAP values for all posts, identifying which shortcuts carry the most predictive weight.

\paragraph{Feature ablation.}
We measure AUC of the full 18-feature model, ablate each shortcut individually and all 6 simultaneously, and stratify the AUC change by gender to test for asymmetric reliance (RQ4).

\paragraph{Causal mediation analysis.}
Following \citet{imai2010general}, for each shortcut $M$ we estimate the Average Causal Mediation Effect (ACME, $G \to M \to Y$), the Average Direct Effect (ADE, $G \to Y$ not through $M$), and the proportion mediated, with 1,000 bootstrap replications for 95\% CIs.
The structural causal model posits $G \to S \to E \to \hat{Y}$ (shortcut path) alongside $G \to C \to E \to \hat{Y}$ (clinical signal path) and a potential direct path $G \to \hat{Y}$.

\section{Results}

\subsection{\textsc{Identify}: Shortcut Detection}

Individual feature AUCs range from 0.50 (\texttt{question\_density}) to 0.64 (\texttt{fp\_singular}).
The combined 18-feature logistic model achieves AUC = $0.713 \pm 0.005$.
MI decomposition identifies 6 features with MI drop $>$24\% when conditioned on gender (Table~\ref{tab:shortcuts}).

\texttt{fp\_singular} has the highest standalone AUC (0.640) and the second-highest MI drop (37.5\%), making it the single most exploitable shortcut.

\paragraph{Control validation.}
Two controls behave as expected: \texttt{negation} (MI drop 0.6\%) and \texttt{cognitive\_processing} (7.9\%).
However, \texttt{past\_tense} (40.0\%) and \texttt{absolutist} (32.7\%) are unexpectedly flagged---evidence that gender confounding in mental health text is deeper than anticipated, even features not typically considered gendered carry substantial gender-mediated information.

\paragraph{Layer-wise probing.}
Figure~\ref{fig:layerwise} shows probing AUC at each of the 7 extraction points (embedding + 6 transformer layers).
All shortcut features jump from chance ($\sim$0.50) to near-peak AUC at Layer~1, remaining flat thereafter.
Gender follows a similar trajectory (0.953 at L1, U-shaped dip to 0.934 at L5, recovery to 0.959 at L6).
The binary label, by contrast, builds slowly across layers (0.780 at L1 $\to$ 0.798 at L6).
Notably, \texttt{question\_density} is the only feature that continues to increase with depth (0.938 $\to$ 0.979), suggesting it is a learned contextual property rather than a surface cue.
The alignment of shortcut curves with gender rather than the label supports the view that shortcuts encode demographic signal, not clinical content.

\subsection{\textsc{Quantify}: Attribution \& Mediation}

\paragraph{SHAP attribution.}
A logistic model on the 6 shortcuts achieves AUC = 0.680.
SHAP analysis reveals \texttt{fp\_singular} accounts for 51.3\% of total $|\text{SHAP}|$ mass, followed by \texttt{emotional\_feeling} (17.1\%) and \texttt{post\_length} (15.0\%).
The dominance of a single feature---over half of total attribution---confirms \texttt{fp\_singular} as the primary shortcut.

\paragraph{Ablation.}
Removing all 6 shortcuts from the 18-feature model drops AUC from 0.7129 to 0.6839 ($\Delta = -0.0291$, $-4.1$\%).
Gender-stratified analysis reveals \texttt{fp\_singular} ablation hurts female predictions $3\times$ more than male (asymmetry = +0.0068); \texttt{post\_length} shows the reverse pattern (asymmetry = +0.0042 toward male), consistent with known gendered writing-length distributions.

\paragraph{Causal mediation.}
The total gender $\to$ label effect is $+0.259$ on the probability scale (females are $\sim$26 pp more likely to receive a positive label).
All 6 shortcuts are statistically significant mediators ($p < 0.05$ via bootstrap CI).
\texttt{fp\_singular} alone mediates $\sim$13.5\% of this effect (ACME $= +0.035$, 95\% CI $[+0.032, +0.038]$); the 6 shortcuts combined account for $\sim$25--30\% of the indirect path (Table~\ref{tab:mediation}).

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{RQ1: Shortcut identification.}
The MI decomposition with built-in controls reveals 6 gender-confounded shortcuts.
The unexpected flagging of 2 control features (\texttt{past\_tense} at 40.0\%, \texttt{absolutist} at 32.7\%) demonstrates that gender confounding in mental health text is deeper than anticipated: even features chosen precisely because they were \textit{not} expected to be gendered carry substantial gender-mediated signal.
Men and women describe distress using systematically different linguistic strategies, making the confound pervasive.

\paragraph{RQ2: Representation encoding.}
All shortcuts reach near-peak probing AUC by Layer~1 and track the gender encoding trajectory rather than the label's gradual build-up.
This suggests shortcuts reflect surface demographic signal already present in the static embeddings, amplified at the first transformer layer, rather than task-relevant clinical content that emerges deeper in the network.
The one exception, \texttt{question\_density}, increases with depth (0.938 $\to$ 0.979), hinting that question usage is a contextual property that the model learns rather than a shallow lexical cue.

\paragraph{RQ3: Causal mediation.}
\texttt{fp\_singular} dominates both attribution (51.3\% SHAP mass) and mediation (13.5\% of the gender $\to$ label effect).
The 6 shortcuts together account for $\sim$25--30\% of the indirect path.
Because ablating all 6 reduces the 18-feature AUC by only 4.1\%, the remaining gender information likely flows through unmeasured channels---consistent with the depth of confounding revealed by the controls.

\paragraph{RQ4: Gender asymmetry.}
Gender-stratified ablation shows that \texttt{fp\_singular} removal hurts female predictions $3\times$ more than male (asymmetry $= +0.0068$).
This asymmetry has direct fairness implications: any classifier built on these representations will be disproportionately influenced by shortcut features for female-authored text, potentially inflating female false-positive rates.

\paragraph{Implications for debiasing.}
Our results indicate that feature-level debiasing is necessary but insufficient.
The pervasive confounding revealed by control features, together with the modest AUC change from ablation, implies that removing specific shortcuts cannot eliminate gender bias.
Representation-level interventions---adversarial training, counterfactual data augmentation, or architectural constraints---are needed.

\paragraph{Connection to dataset auditing.}
G-AUDIT \citep{drenkow2025gaudit} flags shortcut risk at the dataset level.
Our \textsc{Identify}--\textsc{Locate}--\textsc{Quantify} pipeline complements this by tracing risk into model representations, quantifying causal weight, and revealing fairness-relevant asymmetries---bridging the gap between dataset-level detection and model-level diagnosis.

\section{Conclusion}

We present a three-block shortcut audit---\textsc{Identify}, \textsc{Locate}, \textsc{Quantify}---applied to frozen DistilRoBERTa-base on a gender-balanced mental health dataset.
Six gender-correlated writing-style features are identified as shortcuts, with \texttt{fp\_singular} as the dominant one (37.5\% MI drop, 51.3\% SHAP mass, 13.5\% mediation).
Layer-wise probing shows shortcuts are encoded from the first transformer layer and track gender rather than the label.
Two of four controls are unexpectedly flagged, revealing pervasive confounding.
Female predictions are $3\times$ more sensitive to shortcut removal, demonstrating gender-asymmetric reliance.
These findings motivate representation-level debiasing strategies and fairness-aware evaluation for mental health NLP.

\section*{Limitations}

We audit only DistilRoBERTa-base; results may differ for domain-adapted (MentalBERT) or larger models (GPT-4).
We use a single Reddit dataset (Mindset); cross-corpus generalization is untested.
Gender labels are binary and self-disclosed; non-binary populations are excluded.
We probe frozen pre-trained representations without fine-tuning, which may amplify or attenuate shortcut reliance.
Probing classifiers are linear, potentially underestimating nonlinear encoding.
Lexicons are curated open-source word lists, not validated instruments.

\section*{Ethics Statement}

This work aims to identify and characterize bias in mental health NLP with the goal of improving fairness.
We use only publicly available Reddit data with self-disclosed demographics.
No clinical decisions were made.
We acknowledge that our binary gender framework does not capture the full spectrum of gender identity.
